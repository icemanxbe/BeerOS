# Troubleshooting and Off-Flavors

Every off-flavor and process problem in this document is broken down the same way, because that's the actual skill of troubleshooting a beer: it isn't memorizing a list of smells, it's reasoning through a chain.

- **WHY** — the chemical or biological root cause. What compound is it, and what mechanism produces it?
- **WHAT** — what it actually tastes/smells like, so you can recognize it in a glass.
- **WHEN** — where in the process it originates, and when it typically becomes noticeable (which are often two very different points in time).
- **ACTION** — what to do about it. Critically, this document is honest about which off-flavors can be *fixed* in the current batch and which can only be *prevented* in the next one. Most of them are the latter. Beer is not like a sauce you can adjust on the stove — by the time you can taste most of these compounds, the batch that made them is already finished fermenting.
- **CONSEQUENCE** — what happens if the root cause isn't addressed and the same mistake repeats in future batches.

If you take one thing from this document, take this: **diagnosis beats reaction.** Tasting "something's off" and guessing at random fixes wastes ingredients and time. Identifying the specific compound, tracing it to a specific point in your process, and changing that one variable next time is the entire game.

## Read this first: context is everything

Before you treat any of the characteristics below as a flaw, check what style you're actually brewing. The single biggest mistake in home-brew troubleshooting is pattern-matching a flavor to "off-flavor" without asking whether the style being brewed *wants* that flavor. The same compound can be a serious defect in one beer and the entire reason a style exists in another:

- **Clove and banana (4-vinyl guaiacol, isoamyl acetate)** are the defining character of German hefeweizen and many Belgian styles, produced on purpose by specific "phenolic" (POF+) yeast strains. The identical clove compound in a clean American pale ale means an infection or the wrong yeast strain got used.
- **Haze** is a defect in a pilsner or a clean American lager (where it signals incomplete conversion, chill haze, or poor flocculation) but is a deliberately engineered feature of New England IPA, built from protein-rich adjuncts, specific yeast strains, and hop polyphenols, with minimal fining or filtration.
- **Sourness, funk, and "horse blanket" phenolics (Brettanomyces, Lactobacillus)** are spoilage organisms in a clean lager or a hop-forward IPA, and simultaneously the entire point of a Belgian lambic, American wild ale, or many farmhouse saisons, where they're pitched deliberately.
- **Fruity esters** that would read as "yeast stress" in a clean American ale are simply what Belgian and English strains are selected to produce.

So before working through the tables below, ask: *does this style's guidelines actively call for this characteristic?* If yes, it's not a flaw — it's a fermentation outcome that needs tuning (more/less of the desired thing), not eliminating. Everything past this point assumes you've already ruled that out and you're looking at something the style genuinely doesn't want.

## Off-flavors in detail

### Diacetyl (buttery / butterscotch)

- **WHY.** Diacetyl (2,3-butanedione) is a vicinal diketone yeast produces as a side effect of synthesizing the amino acid valine. The direct precursor is alpha-acetolactate, which yeast excretes into the beer; alpha-acetolactate then oxidizes into diacetyl outside the cell. Healthy, actively metabolizing yeast normally reabsorbs diacetyl afterward and reduces it to flavorless compounds. Problems happen when either (a) that reabsorption never finishes, or (b) something is actively producing extra diacetyl. Underpitched or nutrient-starved yeast has to divide more times to reach a working population, and each division event is associated with more valine synthesis and more diacetyl output — so underpitching is a genuine root cause, not an old wives' tale. Separately, contamination by lactic acid bacteria (particularly *Pediococcus*) produces diacetyl directly as a metabolic byproduct, at levels far beyond what stressed yeast alone would generate; this is why persistent, heavy diacetyl in a beer that "should" have cleaned up is a classic sign of infection rather than a fermentation-management mistake.
- **WHAT.** Buttery or butterscotch, often compared directly to the flavor of movie-theater popcorn. A "slick" or oily mouthfeel sometimes accompanies it.
- **WHEN.** Produced throughout active fermentation as alpha-acetolactate; the conversion to actual diacetyl and its reabsorption both continue into the final days of fermentation and beyond if given the chance. It becomes noticeable at tasting/packaging if it hasn't been cleaned up, or later in the bottle/keg if the source is ongoing bacterial contamination rather than a one-time fermentation byproduct.
- **ACTION.** If the beer is still on the yeast (primary not yet finished, or not yet cold-crashed and packaged), a **diacetyl rest** — holding or raising the temperature a few degrees for 1–3 days while the yeast is still in suspension — often still resolves it, because the yeast are still metabolically active and will keep scrubbing it. This is standard practice for lagers. Once the beer has been packaged (bottled/kegged and carbonated), there's no yeast left to clean it up — reabsorption physically cannot happen without living, active yeast in contact with the beer, and no amount of time will fix it after that point. To prevent it happening again: pitch an adequate cell count (use a starter or the correct number of dry-yeast packets for your batch size and gravity — see `../math/calculators-and-formulas.md` for pitch-rate math), aerate/oxygenate the wort properly before pitching, don't rush or artificially truncate fermentation, and don't cold-crash and package the moment gravity stabilizes — give the yeast a few extra days at fermentation temperature first. If diacetyl keeps showing up despite correct pitching and a proper rest, review sanitation for a *Pediococcus* infection.
- **CONSEQUENCE of inaction.** If underpitching or nutrient deficiency is the real cause and nothing changes, every future batch with that same yeast-handling habit will show the same buttery character, and it will get worse in high-gravity beers where yeast stress is already elevated. If the cause is bacterial and sanitation isn't addressed, the contamination can spread to other equipment (especially plastic, which scratches and harbors bacteria) and start showing up in otherwise well-managed batches too.

### Acetaldehyde (green apple)

- **WHY.** Acetaldehyde is the direct chemical precursor to ethanol — yeast produces it as a normal intermediate step and then reduces it further into alcohol. In a healthy, complete fermentation, essentially all of it gets converted onward and reabsorbed; what makes it an off-flavor is process, not an unusual chemical event. It also has a second source: post-fermentation oxidation, where dissolved oxygen reacts with ethanol already in the beer and converts a small amount of it back into acetaldehyde, and a third: a byproduct associated with weak or autolyzing yeast unable to complete normal metabolism.
- **WHAT.** Green apple — specifically freshly cut or bruised apple — sometimes described as latex or nail-polish-adjacent at higher concentrations. It's often mistaken for "the beer isn't done yet," which, when caught early, it usually isn't.
- **WHEN.** Present throughout fermentation as a completely normal, transient intermediate. It becomes a genuine flaw specifically when the beer is removed from the yeast, cold-crashed, or packaged **before** fermentation and the yeast's own cleanup phase have finished — the acetaldehyde simply never gets converted onward. It can also reappear post-packaging via oxidation, which is a separate mechanism from the fermentation-timing issue.
- **ACTION.** If fermentation is still active or was only recently finished and the beer hasn't been packaged, give it more time at fermentation temperature — this is directly fixable because the yeast, still present and active, will finish the job it was already doing. Don't rush a beer to bottle/keg the moment gravity looks stable; confirm with two hydrometer readings a few days apart. Once packaged, acetaldehyde from a rushed fermentation is not fixable — it will fade extremely slowly if at all. If the source is oxidation rather than a rushed ferment, there is no fix; see the Oxidation section. Prevention: don't rush fermentation schedules, pitch enough healthy yeast, and avoid oxygen pickup at every step after fermentation begins.
- **CONSEQUENCE of inaction.** Repeatedly packaging on a fixed calendar schedule rather than confirmed gravity stability will keep producing green-apple beer, and the problem gets worse, not better, with styles fermented at cooler temperatures (lagers) where yeast cleanup naturally takes longer.

### DMS — dimethyl sulfide (cooked corn / vegetable)

- **WHY.** Malt contains S-methylmethionine (SMM), a compound produced during malting, especially in lightly-kilned malts like Pilsner malt (darker, more heavily kilned malts contain much less, since kilning drives SMM off before it ever reaches your kettle). During mashing and boiling, heat converts SMM into DMS. A vigorous, rolling, uncovered boil is what actually removes it — the boil's turbulence carries DMS vapor out of the kettle with the steam. A boil that's too gentle, a lid left on (which traps and condenses the steam, dripping DMS-laden condensate straight back into the wort), or too short a boil time will all leave excess DMS behind. Cooling speed matters too: DMS continues to form from remaining SMM at temperatures as low as roughly 60°C/140°F, so a slow chill gives the wort extended time sitting in that conversion range with no boil-off happening anymore to remove what's forming.
- **WHAT.** Cooked corn, creamed corn, or cooked/canned vegetables. Sometimes described as tomato-juice-like at lower intensities.
- **WHEN.** The precursor (SMM) comes from the malt itself and converts during mashing and boiling; the critical prevention window is the boil and the immediate post-boil chill. It's noticeable from the first taste after fermentation — DMS isn't something that develops later, though normal ale fermentation does scrub out some residual DMS via CO2 off-gassing (lagers, fermented cooler and often less vigorously, are historically more prone to retaining it, which is part of why the flaw is more strongly associated with pale lagers than ales).
- **ACTION.** This is not fixable after the fact — DMS that's in a finished, packaged beer is staying there. Prevention: boil vigorously and uncovered for a full 60–90 minutes (a 60-minute boil removes roughly two-thirds of DMS precursor, a 90-minute boil closer to 80%, so longer helps meaningfully for DMS-prone malt bills), don't put a lid on the kettle, and chill the wort as fast as practical after flame-out rather than letting it coast down slowly. This matters most with high proportions of Pilsner or other pale, lightly-kilned base malt.
- **CONSEQUENCE of inaction.** Any recipe leaning on a large proportion of Pilsner malt (classic pilsners, helles, and similar) will keep producing corn-like off-flavor every time it's boiled too gently or chilled too slowly, regardless of how good the rest of the process is — this one is entirely process-driven and won't improve with better yeast, water, or hops.

### Fusel alcohols ("hot" / solvent character)

- **WHY.** Fusel (higher) alcohols are produced by yeast as part of normal amino acid metabolism, but production scales up sharply under stress: fermentation temperatures too high for the strain, underpitching (which forces more cell division and growth, and fusel production tracks with cell growth), excessive free amino nitrogen (FAN) driving overactive metabolism, and the general osmotic/alcohol stress of high-gravity fermentations all increase output. It is fundamentally a "too much yeast stress, for too much of the fermentation" problem.
- **WHAT.** A hot, boozy, solvent-like harshness — often described as "burning" on the palate independent of actual ABV, or reminiscent of rubbing alcohol/nail polish remover at high concentrations. Distinct from a beer that's simply strong; a well-fermented high-ABV beer can feel warming without being harsh.
- **WHEN.** Produced during active fermentation, driven by temperature and pitch-rate decisions made at or shortly after pitching. Becomes noticeable at first taste and, unlike some of the other compounds here, does not really go away — long conditioning can mellow the perception slightly as other flavors round out, but the fusels themselves remain.
- **ACTION.** Not fixable in the current batch — there is no post-fermentation step that removes fusel alcohols. For future batches: pitch an adequate, correctly sized yeast population (fusel production correlates with how much the yeast population has to grow after pitching, so starting closer to the target cell count reduces growth-driven fusel output), control fermentation temperature within the strain's recommended range (especially avoiding temperature spikes in the first 24–48 hours, when most cell growth happens), and for high-gravity beers, consider staggered nutrient additions and a lower starting pitch temperature to reduce stress. Extended aging can soften the perception somewhat but is not a real fix.
- **CONSEQUENCE of inaction.** High-gravity styles (barleywines, imperial stouts, strong Belgians) brewed repeatedly with underpitched yeast or uncontrolled fermentation temperature will consistently taste hot and boozy rather than warming and smooth, and the problem gets worse as gravity climbs, since both yeast stress and alcohol stress increase together.

### Chlorophenol (Band-Aid / medicinal / plastic)

- **WHY.** Chlorine or chloramine present in tap water (or residual chlorine-based sanitizer that wasn't fully rinsed off equipment) reacts with phenolic compounds naturally present in malt, forming chlorophenols. This is a water/sanitation chemistry problem, not a yeast or fermentation problem.
- **WHAT.** Band-Aid, plastic, medicine-cabinet, or mouthwash-like. Often the first off-flavor new brewers on municipal chlorinated water encounter, and it can be mistaken for a "clove" or "spicy" character by an unpracticed palate, which matters because the fix is completely different (see the distinction below).
- **WHEN.** The chlorine/phenol reaction can happen at any point chlorinated water and malt-derived phenolics are together — during mashing/sparging if brewing liquor is chlorinated, or during cleaning/sanitizing if a chlorine-based sanitizer (e.g., unrinsed bleach solution) contacts the beer or wort. Noticeable from first taste; it does not fade with time.
- **ACTION.** Not fixable once present. Prevention: remove chlorine and chloramine from brewing water before use — a carbon filter is effective against both, while simply letting water sit out or boiling only reliably removes free chlorine, not chloramine (which is more commonly used by municipal utilities today specifically because it's more persistent); alternatively, campden tablets (potassium or sodium metabisulfite) neutralize both almost instantly. Also make sure any chlorine-based sanitizer (bleach) is thoroughly rinsed from equipment — this is one of the reasons most modern homebrewers use no-rinse sanitizers like Star San instead.
- **Distinguishing chlorophenol from Brettanomyces/wild-yeast phenolics:** both can present as "Band-Aid," but the surrounding character tells them apart. Chlorophenol tends to be a fairly one-dimensional plastic/medicinal/antiseptic note with nothing else going on around it, and it traces cleanly to a water or sanitation source. *Brettanomyces* character (see the Infection section) brings the Band-Aid-like 4-ethylphenol alongside a broader, more complex set of barnyard, horse-blanket, leather, or earthy notes from other compounds Brett produces at the same time — it rarely shows up as an isolated, clean plastic note. If you taste Band-Aid *and* farmyard funk together, suspect Brett or wild contamination; if you taste Band-Aid alone and you're on municipal water, suspect chlorine/chloramine first.
- **CONSEQUENCE of inaction.** Every batch brewed with unfiltered chlorinated/chloraminated tap water will carry this flaw, and it will not go away with better yeast handling, better recipes, or more experience — it's purely a water-treatment gap.

### 4-vinyl guaiacol (clove) — desirable or flaw depending on strain and style

- **WHY.** 4-vinyl guaiacol (4VG) is produced when yeast's ferulic acid decarboxylase (FDC1 gene) enzyme acts on ferulic acid, a compound present in the bran of wheat and barley and released into the wort during mashing (a dedicated "ferulic acid rest," a short mash rest around 40–43°C/104–109°F, is sometimes used specifically to liberate more of it). Only yeast strains with an active FDC1 gene — the "POF+" (phenolic off-flavor positive) phenotype — can perform this conversion. Most standard brewing ale and virtually all lager strains are POF- and simply can't produce 4VG regardless of how much ferulic acid is available; classic German Weissbier strains and many Belgian strains are POF+ by design.
- **WHAT.** A clove-like, spicy phenolic character, sometimes described alongside pepper in Belgian contexts.
- **WHEN.** Formed during active fermentation as the yeast's FDC1 enzyme works on ferulic acid liberated during mashing. Noticeable from the beer's first taste onward, and doesn't fade with conditioning.
- **ACTION — this is entirely context-dependent, not a universal flaw.** In a German hefeweizen, dunkelweizen, or many Belgian styles, clove character is desired and often deliberately amplified (cooler fermentation temperatures shift the yeast's balance toward more clove and less banana ester; a ferulic acid rest boosts available precursor). If you want more of it: use a POF+ strain appropriate to style, ferment cooler within that strain's range, and consider a ferulic acid mash rest. If clove character shows up **unwanted** in a beer meant to be clean (an American ale, a lager, anything not calling for phenolics), the cause is almost always either (a) the wrong strain got pitched, or (b) contamination by a wild POF+ yeast. There's no way to remove 4VG from a finished batch — the fix is entirely about next time: confirm you're pitching the strain you think you are, and review sanitation if a "clean" fermentation keeps producing unwanted phenolics.
- **CONSEQUENCE of inaction.** If unwanted clove character is due to a wild POF+ yeast contaminating your equipment or yeast bank, it will keep turning up in otherwise clean beers until the contamination source (commonly plastic equipment, an old yeast culture, or airborne wild yeast in a fermentation space also used for Belgian/wheat brewing) is identified and addressed.

### Autolysis (rubbery / sulfurous / "meaty")

- **WHY.** After fermentation finishes, dead and dying yeast cells settle into a layer of trub/lees at the bottom of the fermenter. Left long enough, especially at warm temperatures, the cells' own internal enzymes break down their cell walls and membranes (autolysis), releasing fatty acids, sulfur compounds, and amino acids/nucleotides into the beer. This is a slow process under normal conditions but accelerates significantly with time and warmth — it's specifically an "extended primary at warm temperature, sitting on a full yeast cake" problem, not something that happens over a normal 1–3 week primary fermentation.
- **WHAT.** Rubbery, sulfurous, or "meaty"/brothy notes; sometimes described as a harsh, bitter "yeast bite" alongside the sulfur/meat character.
- **WHEN.** Fermentation itself doesn't cause it — it's a storage-condition problem that develops during extended time on the yeast cake after fermentation has already completed, most commonly in beers left in primary for many weeks to months at room temperature "because there was no rush to bottle it." It becomes noticeable gradually the longer the beer sits on the cake.
- **ACTION.** Not fixable once autolysis flavors are in the beer. Prevention: don't leave beer sitting on a large volume of trub/dead yeast for extended periods at warm temperature — rack off the yeast cake (to a secondary vessel, or package) within a few weeks of fermentation finishing, and if extended cold conditioning or lagering is planned, do it cold (near 0–4°C/32–39°F), since low temperature dramatically slows the autolysis process even with yeast still present.
- **CONSEQUENCE of inaction.** Any habit of "set and forget" primary fermentation for months at room temperature will eventually produce autolysis character, and it gets worse the longer the beer sits — this is a purely time-and-temperature-driven problem that will recur in every batch treated the same way.

### Oxidation (wet cardboard / papery / sherry-like)

- **WHY.** After fermentation, dissolved oxygen reacts with unsaturated fatty acids and other compounds carried over from malt and hops, producing lipid-oxidation byproducts — trans-2-nonenal is the most notorious, specifically linked to the "wet cardboard" descriptor. This is worth contrasting directly with wort aeration: oxygen is actively **wanted**, even required, in the wort *before* fermentation starts, because yeast need it to synthesize the sterols and unsaturated fatty acids that keep their cell membranes healthy through fermentation. The moment fermentation is underway, that need disappears, and from then on, oxygen is purely a liability — every subsequent contact with air (racking, transferring, bottling, insufficiently purged kegs, loose-fitting caps, prolonged storage) is a chance to introduce it and start these reactions.
- **WHAT.** Wet cardboard or papery character in most beers; in stronger or darker beers, oxidation often reads more like sherry, toffee, or dried-fruit notes rather than cardboard specifically. At low levels it can just present as a general "dullness" — flavors that seem muted or flattened compared to how the beer tasted fresh.
- **WHEN.** The reactions happen after fermentation, and the flavor typically develops progressively during packaging, storage, and aging — it's rarely present at kegging/bottling time and instead shows up over the following weeks to months, worsening the longer the beer sits, especially at warm storage temperatures (heat accelerates the oxidation reactions).
- **ACTION.** Not fixable — there's no way to reverse oxidation once it's occurred. Prevention centers on minimizing oxygen pickup at every step after fermentation: rack/transfer beer gently to avoid splashing, purge racking equipment and receiving vessels with CO2, fill bottles/kegs to minimize headspace air, cap/seal promptly, and keep finished beer cold (cold storage slows the oxidation reactions, extending the beer's fresh window even if it can't stop them entirely).
- **CONSEQUENCE of inaction.** Any batch handled carelessly during transfer/packaging will taste noticeably fresher right after packaging than a few weeks later, and every subsequent batch treated the same way will follow the same fade — hoppy, pale styles especially, since their delicate hop aroma is often the first casualty of oxidation, well before cardboard notes become obvious.

### Infection — wild yeast and bacterial contamination

Multiple distinct organisms cause different symptoms; treating "infection" as one thing leads to the wrong fix. In every case: not fixable in the current batch (beyond accepting or working with the character, in styles where that's viable), and prevention is entirely about sanitation and process discipline.

- **Lactic sourness — WHY:** Lactic acid bacteria, chiefly *Lactobacillus* and *Pediococcus*, ferment sugars into lactic acid. **WHAT:** A clean, direct tartness, often compared to yogurt or sour fruit — not sharp/vinegary. **WHEN:** Can establish itself any time wort or beer is exposed to the bacteria and given time/temperature to grow — commonly from inadequate sanitation, or deliberately in kettle-soured and mixed-fermentation styles. **ACTION:** In a beer that wasn't meant to be sour, there's no fixing it after the fact; prevent via sanitation discipline and, if intentionally souring, controlled inoculation rather than ambient/wild exposure. **CONSEQUENCE:** Persistent contamination sources (plastic equipment with fine scratches, old tubing, an unclean plate chiller) will keep souring subsequent "clean" batches until identified and replaced/sanitized.
- **Acetic sourness — WHY:** *Acetobacter* converts ethanol into acetic acid, but — and this is the important mechanistic detail — it needs **oxygen present** to do it; *Acetobacter* essentially cannot produce meaningful acetic acid in an anaerobic, sealed fermenter. **WHAT:** Sharp, vinegar-like sourness, distinct from lactic acid's cleaner tartness. **WHEN:** Typically develops in beer that's had both bacterial exposure and oxygen exposure — an open or loosely sealed vessel, a beer left with excess headspace, or a stuck airlock allowing air infiltration, especially at warm temperatures (*Acetobacter* favors roughly 24–29°C/75–85°F). **ACTION:** Not fixable; prevent by keeping fermenters and packaging sealed and minimizing oxygen contact, on top of normal sanitation. **CONSEQUENCE:** Recurs in any setup that regularly exposes beer to both airborne bacteria and oxygen — commonly loose-fitting lids, poorly sealed spigots, or extended time in a vessel with a lot of headspace.
- **Brettanomyces character — WHY:** *Brettanomyces* yeast produces 4-ethylphenol and 4-ethylguaiacol via a similar decarboxylation-then-reduction pathway to the 4VG mechanism above, plus a range of other compounds. **WHAT:** Horse blanket, barnyard, leather, and sometimes smoky notes — "funk." **WHEN:** Can take considerably longer to develop than bacterial souring, sometimes weeks to months, since Brett tends to work slowly, especially on the residual dextrins and complex sugars regular brewing yeast leaves behind. **ACTION/CONTEXT:** In a clean lager, IPA, or other non-funky style, unwanted Brett character is a flaw with no fix — only prevention via sanitation and dedicated equipment (many brewers keep separate gear entirely for wild/sour projects, since Brett is notoriously difficult to fully clean out of plastic and other porous surfaces). In American wild ales, some farmhouse saisons, and lambic-family styles, Brett is pitched deliberately and the same character is the entire point. **CONSEQUENCE:** Cross-contamination from shared equipment is the single most common way "clean" homebrew setups end up with a persistent, unwanted Brett problem across many future batches.
- **Pellicle formation:** A visible film on the beer's surface is a strong visual signal of bacterial or wild-yeast activity (particularly *Acetobacter* and *Brettanomyces*, which form pellicles more readily in oxygen-exposed conditions) — but its **absence** doesn't rule out infection, since these organisms don't always form a visible pellicle, especially in a sealed, low-oxygen fermenter. Treat a pellicle as a red flag worth investigating, not as the only diagnostic.
- **Gushing / unexpected over-carbonation from wild yeast — WHY:** Ordinary brewing yeast can't ferment complex dextrins that survive normal fermentation — that's exactly why a beer's final gravity stabilizes where it does. Wild/diastatic strains (notably *Saccharomyces cerevisiae* var. *diastaticus*, carrying the STA1 gene) secrete glucoamylase, an enzyme that breaks those dextrins down into simple, fermentable sugars the yeast can then keep consuming, long after the beer was "finished" and packaged. **WHAT:** Excess, sometimes violent carbonation (gushing) when a bottle or can is opened, plus the loss of body/sweetness the dextrins were providing, and often accompanying off-flavors. **WHEN:** Fermentation appears to finish normally and the beer is packaged at what looks like a stable final gravity; the diastatic activity then continues slowly in the package over subsequent weeks to months, building CO2 pressure the packaging wasn't designed for. **ACTION:** Not fixable once it's happening — the pressure buildup is ongoing and packaged bottles/cans can become a genuine safety hazard (risk of shattering). Prevent via strict sanitation (STA1+ wild yeast is a known, named brewing-industry contamination risk, not a hypothetical) and, if repeatedly suspected, a forced-fermentation test with beer samples to check whether gravity keeps dropping well past where the pitched strain should have stopped. **CONSEQUENCE:** Recurring contamination will produce a string of over-carbonated, gushing, thin-tasting batches and creates a real safety risk from over-pressurized packaging; any equipment or yeast culture suspected of diastatic contamination should be discarded/replaced rather than reused.

### Astringency (puckering / tannic / tea-like)

- **WHY.** Grain husks contain polyphenols (tannins) and silica compounds that become more soluble — and therefore more easily extracted into the wort — under higher pH and higher temperature. Astringency results from over-extracting these compounds: sparging with too much water relative to what the grain bed can cleanly release (over-sparging, typically identified by continuing to collect runnings after their gravity has dropped below roughly 1.008–1.010, or after sparge runnings' pH has climbed above about 6.0), sparge water that's too hot (above roughly 168°F/76°C), mash or sparge pH drifting too high, or, in extract brewing, steeping specialty grains too hot, too long, or crushing them too aggressively before steeping (the same husk-tannin mechanism applies even without a true mash).
- **WHAT.** A drying, puckering, tannic sensation — often compared to over-steeped black tea or the feeling of biting into a grape skin. It's a mouthfeel/tactile sensation more than a "flavor" in the aroma-compound sense, though it's often accompanied by a harsh, grainy bitterness.
- **WHEN.** Introduced during mashing/sparging (all-grain) or grain steeping (extract brewing) — entirely a pre-boil process issue. Noticeable in the finished beer from first taste; it doesn't develop later and doesn't fade with conditioning.
- **ACTION.** Not fixable once it's in the beer. Prevention: stop sparging (or switch to topping up with plain water) once runnings' gravity drops to around 1.008–1.010, or monitor runnings pH and stop before it exceeds roughly 6.0; keep sparge water at or below about 168°F/76°C; keep mash pH in the standard 5.2–5.6 range; and in extract brewing, steep specialty grains at the recommended temperature (well below a full mash temperature for character/color-only grains) for the recommended time rather than boiling them or leaving them in indefinitely. Handle the grain bed gently generally — don't stir or agitate vigorously once sparging starts, since disturbing the bed can churn up husk material into suspension.
- **CONSEQUENCE of inaction.** Any recurring sparge habit that pushes past the safe gravity/pH cutoff, or any recipe that steeps specialty grains too hot in extract brewing, will keep producing the same harsh, tannic character batch after batch, and it tends to get worse as batch size or grain bill scales up if sparge technique doesn't scale with it.

### Excessive/harsh bitterness vs. clean bitterness

- **WHY.** Harsh, unpleasant bitterness (as opposed to a beer that's simply strongly, but cleanly, bitter) generally traces to one of: genuinely over-hopping relative to the beer's malt backbone and style target, using hops that have oxidized/degraded from poor storage (old, improperly stored hops lose desirable aromatics faster than they lose bittering potential, and the bittering character that's left can taste rougher and less refined), or occasionally poor hop-stand/whirlpool practice that extracts excess plant material and harsh resins alongside the intended bitterness. **On cohumulone specifically:** a long-standing homebrew belief holds that hop varieties with higher cohumulone content (a specific alpha-acid subtype) produce inherently harsher bitterness than low-cohumulone varieties. This claim traces back to a 1972 taste test, but the original test compared beers at substantially different actual IBU levels, confounding the result, and more recent controlled comparisons (including a Brülosophy triangle test and a separate American Homebrewers Association test) found tasters could sometimes tell high- and low-cohumulone beers apart at matched IBU levels, but showed no consistent preference against the high-cohumulone beer — i.e., the "harsher" claim is not well supported by controlled modern testing, and should be treated as debated/likely overstated rather than an established brewing fact.
- **WHAT.** A harsh, lingering, sometimes chemical-tasting bitterness that doesn't fade cleanly on the palate, as opposed to a clean, resinous, or citrusy bitterness that yields naturally to the malt.
- **WHEN.** Introduced at hopping decisions (recipe formulation and boil/whirlpool additions); noticeable from first taste, though a genuinely too-bitter recipe can occasionally mellow slightly with extended conditioning as flavors integrate — not the case for harshness driven by degraded hops, which won't improve with time.
- **ACTION.** Not fixable in a finished batch — bitterness can't be removed from beer after the fact. Prevention: hop to style-appropriate IBU targets rather than by feel, store hops properly (vacuum-sealed, oxygen-purged if possible, refrigerated or frozen) to avoid the rougher character of oxidized hop resins, and don't over-correct with excessive late/whirlpool hop mass without accounting for the added plant material and resin load. Don't chase the cohumulone number as a quality signal — it's a secondary, debated consideration well behind fresh hops and an appropriate IBU target.
- **CONSEQUENCE of inaction.** Repeatedly formulating recipes with excessive IBU relative to malt body, or continuing to brew with old/poorly stored hops, will keep producing beers that taste harshly bitter rather than pleasantly so, regardless of yeast or water adjustments elsewhere in the process.

## Process problems

### Stuck or incomplete fermentation

- **WHY.** True stuck fermentation (as opposed to a beer that has simply finished at its expected gravity) has a handful of real causes: underpitching (too few viable cells to fully attenuate the wort), fermentation temperature too low or a cold crash applied before fermentation actually finished (yeast activity drops sharply outside a strain's tolerance range and can stall out entirely), insufficient wort oxygenation at pitching time (yeast need oxygen pre-fermentation to build the cell membrane components required for healthy, complete fermentation — a callback to the oxidation section's point that oxygen is wanted *before* fermentation and harmful *after*), osmotic/alcohol stress in very high-gravity worts (concentrated sugar and, later, rising alcohol both stress yeast function), and, less commonly in beer than in high-gravity meads, insufficient yeast nutrients (FAN) — a real factor mainly in big, high-OG beers (barleywines, imperial stouts) rather than standard-gravity ales.
- **WHAT/WHEN — diagnosis.** The only reliable way to tell a genuinely stuck fermentation from a beer that's simply *done* is measurement, not guessing from time elapsed or activity level: take a gravity reading with a sanitized hydrometer or refractometer, wait 2–3 days, and take a second reading. If gravity hasn't moved and airlock activity has stopped, compare the reading against the pitched strain's documented attenuation range (cross-reference the strain tables in `../ingredients/yeast.md`) — a stable reading that lands within the strain's normal expected attenuation window for that wort's fermentability is simply a finished beer, not a problem. A stable reading sitting well *above* the strain's expected range (commonly 5–10+ gravity points higher than predicted) is the actual signal of a stuck fermentation.
- **ACTION.** Work through causes roughly least- to most-invasive: gently rouse the yeast back into suspension (swirl or rock the fermenter without introducing oxygen) in case it's flocculated out prematurely while sugar remains; check and, if needed, raise fermentation temperature a few degrees within the strain's tolerance range to reinvigorate activity; if underpitching or nutrient stress is suspected, especially in a high-gravity beer, consider a yeast nutrient addition; if none of that produces movement over another few days, pitch a fresh, appropriately sized batch of yeast (ideally a hardier or more alcohol-tolerant strain for a high-gravity stuck beer) rather than assuming the existing population will recover on its own. If, on the other hand, the stable reading matches the strain's known attenuation range for that wort, there is nothing to fix — that's just the finished beer.
- **CONSEQUENCE of inaction.** A truly stuck fermentation left alone risks producing a beer that's too sweet, under-attenuated, and at elevated risk of infection or unwanted refermentation in the package (residual fermentable sugar sitting around is exactly the substrate wild yeast/bacteria exploit) — and a wrongly-diagnosed "stuck" fermentation that's actually just finished, if force-fed more yeast or nutrient unnecessarily, wastes ingredients without changing the outcome. Repeating the same underpitching/cold/low-oxygenation habits will produce the same stuck or sluggish result in every high-gravity or cold-fermented batch going forward.

### Low mash/brewhouse efficiency

- **WHY.** Efficiency losses stack from several independent sources: a crush that's too coarse (larger grain particles have less surface area for enzymes to act on, leaving starch un-converted or trapped inside intact husk material); insufficient mash temperature or time for the enzymes present to fully convert available starch (conversion depends on both gelatinization, which proceeds faster at higher mash temperatures, and enzyme activity, which denatures faster at higher temperatures — so there's a real balance point, and too short a mash simply doesn't give either process time to finish); sparge technique that doesn't fully rinse residual sugars from the grain bed (too fast a sparge doesn't give diffusion enough contact time; stopping sparge collection too early leaves sugar behind); and dead space in the mash tun or lauter setup that traps a fixed volume of sugar-rich wort that never makes it to the kettle.
- **WHAT.** Consistently lower original gravity than the recipe predicts, given the same grain bill and volume.
- **WHEN.** Every efficiency-loss cause above originates during the mash and lauter/sparge steps — nothing after wort collection can recover lost efficiency (boiling down harder concentrates what's already in the kettle but doesn't add extract that was left behind in the mash tun). It's noticeable immediately, at the first gravity reading of collected wort.
- **ACTION.** This is a diagnosable, fixable-going-forward problem, not a "prevent only" one, in the sense that identifying and correcting the specific cause reliably improves the next batch: check crush consistency (grain should show visible fine granulation of the endosperm with husks still largely intact — if using a shop's mill, consider a home mill you control for consistency); confirm mash temperature is accurate (verify thermometer calibration) and give a full-conversion mash enough time (60 minutes is standard; iodine testing can confirm starch conversion is complete before mashing out); review sparge approach — for fly sparging, a slow, even rinse over 30–60 minutes generally extracts more than a fast one, while for batch sparging, make sure to fully drain and stir between batches; and account for known dead space in the system by adjusting strike/sparge water volumes to compensate, or by topping up with extra sparge water to push trapped wort through. Track efficiency numbers batch to batch (a brewing log/software feature) so you can tell whether a change actually helped rather than guessing.
- **CONSEQUENCE of inaction.** Without correcting the actual cause, a system will consistently underperform its theoretical efficiency batch after batch, forcing either weaker-than-intended beers or compensating with more grain than a recipe calls for — an ongoing cost and a moving target that makes recipe formulation unreliable until the root cause is found.

### Excessive or insufficient carbonation in packaged beer

- **WHY.** Several distinct mechanisms, each needing a different fix:
  - **Priming sugar miscalculation** — using the wrong amount of priming sugar for the batch's actual volume and desired CO2 volumes produces too much or too little carbonation, straightforwardly.
  - **Residual CO2 at priming time** — beer already holds some dissolved CO2 from fermentation, and how much depends on the beer's temperature at the time you're calculating your priming sugar dose (colder beer holds more dissolved CO2 than warmer beer at the same pressure). If a priming calculation assumes a residual CO2 level that doesn't match the beer's actual temperature history, the total carbonation ends up off in either direction. See `../math/calculators-and-formulas.md` for the residual-CO2-by-temperature figures and priming sugar formulas.
  - **Hop creep** — in heavily dry-hopped beers, hops themselves carry enzymes (including amyloglucosidase and beta-glucosidase) that can break down previously "unfermentable" dextrins into simple sugars the yeast can then ferment further, after the beer appeared finished and was already primed/packaged based on a now-outdated final gravity. That extra fermentation adds unplanned CO2 on top of the priming dose.
  - **Infection-driven gushing** — as covered above, diastatic wild yeast or certain bacteria can continue fermenting residual dextrins in the package for the same reason hop creep does, but from a contamination source rather than the hops themselves.
- **WHAT.** Under-carbonation: flat beer, weak or absent head. Over-carbonation: excessive foaming on opening, gushing, or in severe cases pressure high enough to be a safety concern with glass bottles.
- **WHEN.** The root decision happens at packaging (priming sugar dose, chosen based on measured temperature and target CO2 volumes); hop-creep or infection-driven over-carbonation instead develops gradually over the weeks after packaging as the unplanned secondary fermentation proceeds. Under/over-carbonation from simple miscalculation is usually apparent as soon as the beer is conditioned and ready to drink; hop-creep or infection-driven gushers often surprise brewers weeks later, well after the beer seemed stable.
- **ACTION.** For priming/temperature miscalculation: not fixable after packaging for that batch (though a badly under-carbonated batch can sometimes be corrected by adding a small, carefully calculated dose of additional priming sugar if you're confident no significant unaccounted fermentation is still happening — done cautiously, since over-correcting risks over-carbonation or gushers). Prevention: always calculate priming sugar based on the beer's actual temperature at the warmest point it reached during fermentation (not the temperature at packaging), using a proper residual-CO2 table. For hop creep: not fixable once it's happening in the package; prevention means accounting for expected further attenuation in heavily dry-hopped beers before priming — some brewers dry-hop in the fermenter before fermentation is fully finished specifically so any hop-creep-driven attenuation happens before packaging and gravity is measured, rather than after. For infection-driven gushing: see the Infection section — not fixable, sanitation and equipment review are the only prevention.
- **CONSEQUENCE of inaction.** Repeating priming calculations without accounting for actual beer temperature will produce inconsistent carbonation across batches; ignoring hop creep risk in heavily dry-hopped styles (hazy IPAs especially) risks recurring gushers and, in bottles, a genuine breakage/safety hazard; and unaddressed infection sources will keep producing unpredictable over-carbonation until found and eliminated.

### Cloudy/hazy beer when clarity was expected

- **WHY.** Three distinct mechanisms produce haze, and distinguishing them matters because only some are worth "fixing":
  - **Chill haze** — proteins from malt and polyphenols from malt/hops bind together into complexes that are soluble at warm temperature but precipitate out of solution as fine, visible particles when the beer is chilled. Critically, this reaction is **temperature-reversible**: the same beer that looks hazy cold will often clear up again if warmed back up, and repeated chilling and time (cold crashing, extended cold conditioning) encourages these complexes to grow large enough to fully settle out and stay out, rather than re-dissolving on warming.
  - **Incomplete flocculation** — the pitched yeast strain simply hasn't fully dropped out of suspension yet, whether due to strain characteristics (low-flocculation strains, as used deliberately in hefeweizen), insufficient time, insufficient cold conditioning, or fermentation temperature that discouraged normal flocculation behavior.
  - **Starch haze** — incomplete conversion during the mash leaves unconverted starch in the wort, which produces a haze distinct from protein/polyphenol chill haze and, unlike chill haze, will not clear with cold time alone since it isn't a temperature-reversible complex — it reflects an actual mashing shortfall (see Low mash efficiency above for the same root causes: crush, mash temperature/time).
- **WHAT.** Visible cloudiness/haze in a beer style where the guidelines call for bright clarity (pilsners, many lagers, clean pale ales).
- **WHEN.** Chill haze appears specifically when the beer is chilled, and can seem to appear "suddenly" at packaging or serving temperature even though the beer looked clear at fermentation temperature. Flocculation-related haze is present from the end of fermentation onward and improves with time/cold conditioning. Starch haze is present from the moment wort is collected (a competent iodine test at mash-out will catch it before it ever reaches the fermenter) and persists through the whole process regardless of temperature.
- **ACTION.** Chill haze: not exactly "fixable" after the fact in the sense of removing already-precipitated haze, but it is very much manageable — extended cold crashing (days, not hours) and time allow the haze-forming complexes to fully drop out and stay out, and fining agents (e.g., gelatin, which binds and drags down suspended particles) can accelerate this. Incomplete flocculation: give more time and colder temperature for conditioning, or use fining agents; for future batches, pick a higher-flocculating strain if bright clarity matters for the style. Starch haze: not fixable after the fact — it traces back to an incomplete mash, so the fix is entirely for next time (correct crush, mash temperature, and time, confirmed with an iodine test before moving on from the mash).
- **Contrast with NEIPA haze:** in a New England IPA, haze isn't a mistake to chase away — it's a deliberately engineered outcome, built from protein-rich adjuncts (flaked wheat, flaked oats) that supply the same kind of proteins involved in chill haze, specific yeast strains that stay in suspension and interact favorably with those proteins and hop polyphenols, and a brewing process that deliberately skips or minimizes fining and filtration so the haze is preserved rather than dropped out. If you're troubleshooting cloudiness in a NEIPA, you're generally troubleshooting *insufficient* haze/body, not excess — a different problem than everything else in this section.
- **CONSEQUENCE of inaction.** A style expecting bright clarity that's repeatedly packaged straight off a short cold crash, without addressing flocculation or giving chill haze complexes time to fully settle, will keep looking hazy at serving temperature even if it seemed fine at room temperature during fermentation. Recurring starch haze points to a mash process issue that will also be silently costing fermentable extract (see the Low mash efficiency section) — the two problems often show up together.
