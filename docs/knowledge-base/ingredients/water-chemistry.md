# Water Chemistry

## Why water matters

Water is typically **90-95% of finished beer by weight**. It is also the medium the entire mash and boil happen in, so its dissolved mineral content (not its taste as drinking water) governs several things that have nothing to do with how "clean" the water tastes:

- **Mash pH.** The ions in water react with phosphates and other compounds released from malt during mashing, shifting mash pH up or down. Mash pH in turn governs enzyme activity, starch conversion efficiency, extraction of tannins and silicates from grain husks, wort clarity, and how harsh or smooth the finished beer tastes.
- **Enzyme activity.** The starch- and protein-converting enzymes in malt (alpha-amylase, beta-amylase, and others) each have a pH range where they work efficiently; water chemistry is one of the main levers that determines where in that range the mash actually sits.
- **Hop bitterness perception and malt flavor expression.** Independent of anything happening chemically in the mash, certain ions (notably sulfate and chloride) change how a taster's palate perceives bitterness and malt fullness, which is why brewers deliberately adjust water mineral content to suit a beer style even when mash pH is already on target.

Because of this, water chemistry is generally treated as a distinct ingredient category alongside malt, hops, and yeast — not just "the stuff you mix everything else into."

## The key ions and their brewing role

The concentrations below are commonly cited *target/typical ranges* for all-grain homebrewing, drawn from Bru'n Water's water knowledge documentation and John Palmer's *How to Brew*. Treat them as reasonable starting points, not hard limits — real historical brewing water (see below) routinely falls outside them for specific styles.

### Calcium (Ca²⁺)

- **Typical target range:** roughly 50-100 ppm for most ale styles; a minimum of about 40 ppm is commonly recommended even for delicate lagers, mainly to support oxalate precipitation (see below). Ranges up to 150 ppm are used for some styles.
- **Lowers mash pH.** Calcium reacts with phosphates naturally present in malt (and to a lesser degree with malt proteins) in an acid-forming reaction that consumes alkalinity and lowers mash pH. This is one of the two main "hardness" ions used in the Residual Alkalinity concept below.
- **Yeast health and flocculation.** Calcium is a required cofactor for yeast cell function, and most strains need at least roughly 50 mg/L for good flocculation at the end of fermentation. Very high calcium can, however, displace magnesium that yeast also need, so more is not simply always better.
- **Oxalate precipitation.** Malt contains oxalic acid. Calcium combines with it to precipitate insoluble calcium oxalate, which helps prevent beerstone (scale) buildup, oxalate-driven haze, and gushing. Roughly 40 ppm calcium is generally considered enough for this purpose.
- **Hot break / cold break.** During the boil, calcium ions help neutralize and precipitate coagulated proteins (hot break) and continue assisting protein/haze precipitation as wort cools (cold break), contributing to a cleaner, more stable beer.
- **Practical downside of very low calcium:** poorer enzyme stability, weaker flocculation, more beerstone risk, and (per Bru'n Water) borderline suitability even for lagers that otherwise tolerate soft water.

### Magnesium (Mg²⁺)

- **Typical target range:** roughly 0-30 ppm preferred; going much above about 30-50 ppm is generally discouraged.
- **Yeast nutrient in small amounts.** Magnesium is a cofactor for several yeast enzymes and is a genuine, beneficial micronutrient at low concentration — malt itself usually supplies enough that you rarely need to add more on purpose.
- **Harsh/sour/bitter above ~30-50 ppm.** Magnesium sulfate (Epsom salt) in particular is described across sources as producing a harsh, sour, or bitter/astringent note when magnesium climbs well above the preferred range, especially in combination with elevated sulfate. It also contributes acidity to the mash (see Residual Alkalinity), though less strongly per ppm than calcium.
- **Practical guidance:** it's rarely necessary to deliberately add magnesium salts; if the malt bill and water already provide 10-30 ppm, that's normally sufficient.

### Sodium (Na⁺)

- **Typical target range:** brewers commonly aim for well under 100-150 ppm, and historic brewing centers with well-regarded water are typically below ~60 ppm.
- **Mouthfeel / malt sweetness enhancer in small amounts.** At low concentrations (roughly 10-70 ppm), sodium can round out mouthfeel and accentuate a sense of malt sweetness/fullness, similar to how a pinch of salt enhances flavor in cooking.
- **Harsh/minerally at higher levels, especially combined with sulfate or chloride.** Above roughly 100-150 ppm, and particularly when paired with high sulfate, sodium contributes to a harsh, salty, or minerally character that most brewers consider a flaw. Sodium combined with high chloride can push toward an unpleasant "salty" impression rather than a pleasant fuller mouthfeel.
- Sodium does not meaningfully affect mash pH the way calcium and magnesium do.

### Sulfate (SO₄²⁻)

- **Typical target range:** roughly 0-350 ppm depending on style, with amounts above ~150 ppm reserved mainly for aggressively hopped beers; concentrations above ~400 ppm risk astringent, harsh bitterness, and very high levels (roughly 750+ ppm, as in unmodified historical Burton well water) can have a mild laxative effect.
- **Accentuates hop bitterness — a "crisp/dry" perception.** Sulfate doesn't change measured IBUs; it changes how bitterness is perceived, generally making it feel sharper, drier, and more assertive. This is the mineral brewers reach for first when building water for hoppy styles like IPA or English pale ale.
- Contributes mildly to mash acidity but its main brewing role is flavor perception rather than pH.

### Chloride (Cl⁻)

- **Typical target range:** roughly 10-100 ppm as an "ideal" band; when sulfate exceeds ~100 ppm, many sources suggest keeping chloride closer to 50 ppm or below to avoid a harsh combined mineral character.
- **Accentuates malt fullness/sweetness perception.** Chloride tends to round out and soften the palate, emphasizing malt body and a fuller, smoother mouthfeel — the flavor-perception counterpart to sulfate's bitterness-sharpening effect. This is why chloride-forward water is associated with malt-forward styles (English mild, Munich helles, sweeter stouts).

### Bicarbonate / Carbonate (HCO₃⁻ / CO₃²⁻)

- **Raises (or resists lowering) mash pH.** Bicarbonate and carbonate are alkalinity — they neutralize acid. During mashing, malt itself is naturally acidic (dark and specialty malts especially so) and tends to pull mash pH down; alkalinity in the water resists that natural drop, which is why high-alkalinity water is historically associated with dark beer styles (the malt's acidity and the water's alkalinity partially cancel out) and is a liability for pale styles (nothing to counteract it, so mash pH drifts too high).
- **Directly tied to Residual Alkalinity** (below) — RA is essentially "how much of this pH-raising effect is left over after calcium and magnesium hardness partially cancel it out."
- Measured as total alkalinity, usually expressed in ppm as CaCO₃ (or in some regional water reports, as °dH or French degrees — always confirm units before using a number from a water report).

## The Sulfate:Chloride ratio — a useful shorthand, with caveats

Brewers commonly use the sulfate-to-chloride **ratio** (by ppm) as shorthand for the "hoppy/dry vs. malty/round" axis of perceived beer character:

- A ratio near **1:1** is generally considered balanced — neither hop-crisp nor malt-round dominates.
- Ratios well **above 1** (more sulfate than chloride) push perception toward a crisper, drier, more hop-accentuated character. John Palmer's *Water* (with Colin Kaminski) discusses a practical useful range from roughly **0.5:1 up to about 9:1**, noting that outside that band you're usually dealing with an absolute sulfate or chloride level that's already too high or too low to be pleasant on its own.
- Ratios **below 1** (more chloride than sulfate — e.g., 2:1 chloride:sulfate) push perception toward a fuller, maltier, rounder character.

**Important caveat, stated honestly:** the ratio is a widely used mental model, but it is not universally endorsed as sufficient on its own. Brewer's Friend's water chemistry guidance explicitly argues that the same ratio can taste very different depending on the *absolute* concentrations involved (50:50 ppm reads very differently from 300:300 ppm even though both are a 1:1 ratio), so the ratio should be treated as a starting heuristic for style direction, not a precise flavor prediction. Absolute levels of both ions still matter and should stay within the ranges above.

## Residual Alkalinity (RA)

### The concept

Malt is naturally acidic — the phosphates, proteins, and (especially) melanoidins and roasted compounds in malt tend to pull mash pH down into the desirable ~5.2-5.6 range on their own, without any help from water. Alkalinity in the mash water (bicarbonate/carbonate) resists that natural acidification — it acts as a buffer that has to be "used up" neutralizing acid before mash pH can drop further.

At the same time, **calcium and magnesium hardness independently push mash pH down** through their own reaction with malt phosphates (discussed above under Calcium and Magnesium). So a given water's net effect on mash pH is a tug-of-war between:

- alkalinity (pushes pH up / resists the drop), vs.
- calcium + magnesium hardness (pushes pH down)

**Residual Alkalinity (RA)** is the metric that captures the *net* result of that tug-of-war, expressed in the same units as alkalinity (ppm as CaCO₃) so it can be compared directly against a target grist's own acidity. A water with high RA will tend to push mash pH higher than a water with low or negative RA, all else equal — which is why very alkaline water is a real liability for pale beers (nothing to offset it) but can be perfectly appropriate, even beneficial, for dark beers whose roasted malts bring plenty of their own acidity to balance against it.

### The formula

Bru'n Water's water knowledge documentation gives the formula as:

```
RA = Total Alkalinity − (Calcium / 3.5 + Magnesium / 7)
```

with all quantities expressed in **ppm as CaCO₃**. (If calcium and magnesium are measured in ppm as the elemental ion rather than as CaCO₃ equivalents, calcium is divided by 3.5 and magnesium by 7 specifically to convert each into its CaCO₃-equivalent acid-neutralizing contribution before subtracting.) Some other sources present the same idea with slightly different divisors (for example, calcium/2.5 and magnesium/3.3) depending on the exact equivalent-weight convention used — this is a known area where different calculators and texts use marginally different constants. When in doubt, defer to the specific calculator's documented formula rather than mixing constants from different sources.

### Why this is a concept to understand, not a formula to hand-calculate

RA only tells you about the water's own alkalinity/hardness balance. It does **not**, by itself, predict actual mash pH — that also depends heavily on:

- the specific malts in the grist (base malt is only mildly acidic; crystal, roasted, and acidulated malts are substantially more acidic, each with its own characteristic contribution per pound/kg);
- the ratio of water to grain (mash thickness);
- and to a smaller extent, mash temperature.

Full mash-pH prediction models (the ones implemented in Bru'n Water, EZ Water Calculator, and the Brewer's Friend Water Calculator) combine RA-style water math with per-malt acidity/buffering data and grist-ratio math. That combined calculation is genuinely complex — it is not something to do by hand on brew day, and this document does not attempt to reproduce it. See **[../math/calculators-and-formulas.md](../math/calculators-and-formulas.md)** for what this app implements for mash pH and water-adjustment prediction.

**Be honest about the limits of prediction:** even a good calculator's mash pH output is an *estimate* built from generalized malt acidity data, not a guarantee for your specific malt lot, water report, and mash regime. The only way to know your actual mash pH is to measure it — with a calibrated pH meter (using fresh calibration buffers) or with brewing-specific pH strips, taken from a mash sample.

**Mash-temperature vs. room-temperature readings matter.** pH electrodes read differently at mash temperature (roughly 150-155°F / 65-68°C) than at room temperature, because pH itself is temperature-dependent — a solution genuinely becomes more acidic (lower pH reading) as it warms, separately from any electrode calibration effect. Reported offsets between mash-temp and room-temp readings vary by source (commonly cited figures range from about 0.2 to 0.35 pH units, with John Palmer using roughly 0.25 and *Malting and Brewing Science* citing roughly 0.3-0.35), so there isn't one universally agreed-upon exact correction factor. The two practical approaches brewers use are: (1) cool a mash sample down to near room temperature before measuring, and compare against a room-temperature target (commonly ~5.2-5.6), or (2) measure at mash temperature with a meter that has automatic temperature compensation (ATC) and apply an approximate offset — keeping in mind ATC only corrects for the electrode's own temperature response, not for the sample's genuine chemical pH shift with temperature. Cooling the sample first is the more reliable of the two.

## Starting from different water sources

### Tap water

Municipal tap water is treated with chlorine or chloramine as a disinfectant. Both react with phenolic compounds from malt to form chlorophenols, which taste medicinal or like a band-aid (an off-flavor discussed further below) — and chloramine in particular does not boil off the way free chlorine partially does, so it needs active removal.

- **Campden tablets (potassium or sodium metabisulfite)** neutralize both free chlorine and chloramine almost instantly on contact. The commonly cited dosing rate is **one campden tablet per 20 US gallons (~75 L)** of water, or roughly a quarter tablet per 5-gallon batch. As loose powder, this works out to approximately **9 mg per liter of potassium metabisulfite**. Crush the tablet, stir it into the water, and let it sit a few minutes before use. The residual byproducts (a small amount of chloride/sulfate and potassium or sodium) are negligible for brewing purposes at this dose.
- **Activated carbon filtration** is a solid alternative: a carbon block or standard pitcher/inline carbon filter removes free chlorine effectively and, with adequate contact time (carbon block filters are markedly more effective than fast-flow pitcher filters), can reduce chloramine as well. Carbon filtration doesn't touch the underlying mineral content, though — it only addresses the disinfectant residual.
- Tap water otherwise carries a generally unknown and often seasonally variable mineral profile unless you get an actual water quality/consumer confidence report from your utility. That's the core problem with tap water as a brewing base: even after dechlorination, you're brewing with an unknown (or only approximately known) starting mineral profile, which introduces real uncertainty into any pH or flavor prediction.

### RO or distilled water, built up from zero

Reverse osmosis (RO) water is nearly ion-free; distilled water is essentially completely ion-free. Both are a blank slate — brewers deliberately add back specific mineral salts to hit a target profile:

| Salt | Common name | Adds | Typical use |
|---|---|---|---|
| CaSO₄ (dihydrate) | Gypsum | Calcium **and** sulfate | Raise calcium and push sulfate:chloride toward "hoppy" |
| CaCl₂ (dihydrate) | Calcium chloride | Calcium **and** chloride | Raise calcium and push sulfate:chloride toward "malty" |
| MgSO₄ (heptahydrate) | Epsom salt | Magnesium and sulfate | Minor magnesium nutrient boost, or add sulfate without adding calcium |
| NaCl | Table salt (non-iodized, no anti-caking agents) | Sodium and chloride | Mouthfeel/malt sweetness in small doses |
| CaCO₃ | Chalk | Calcium and alkalinity | Raise alkalinity for dark/acidic beers (dissolves poorly in the mash — see note below) |
| NaHCO₃ | Baking soda | Sodium and alkalinity | Raise alkalinity without adding calcium; watch sodium level |

Per Bru'n Water's published salt-addition figures, roughly **1 gram of gypsum in 1 US gallon** adds about **61 ppm calcium and 147 ppm sulfate**; 1 gram of calcium chloride dihydrate in 1 gallon adds about 72 ppm calcium and 127 ppm chloride; 1 gram of Epsom salt in 1 gallon adds about 26 ppm magnesium and 103 ppm sulfate. These are stoichiometric (molecular-weight-based) figures and are consistent across well-documented brewing water calculators, though exact figures can differ slightly by source depending on hydrate form assumed (e.g., calcium chloride dihydrate vs. anhydrous) — always check which hydrate form a given calculator or product label assumes.

A specific technical note on chalk: calcium carbonate dissolves very poorly in water and reacts sluggishly even in the mash, so simply dumping chalk powder into strike water is an unreliable way to raise alkalinity — Kai Troester's research on this (braukaiser.com) found chalk added directly to the mash often doesn't fully dissolve or take effect as predicted. Baking soda (sodium bicarbonate) is more reliably soluble but adds sodium, which has its own upper limit. Some brewers instead use pre-dissolved "chalk water" (CO₂-saturated) or simply accept some residual alkalinity from a blend of RO and tap water rather than dosing solid chalk.

Brewing with RO or distilled water is also commonly paired with a small amount of yeast nutrient / zinc supplementation, since stripping all ions also removes trace zinc that yeast use.

### Why RO + build-from-scratch gives the most control

Starting from RO or distilled water and adding a known, measured quantity of each salt means you know your exact starting ion profile — nothing is left to a municipal water report that may be seasonal, approximate, or simply unavailable in enough detail (few utilities report sulfate, chloride, and alkalinity together in the units brewers need). This is the main reason serious all-grain brewers who care about reproducibility (brewing the same beer the same way every time) build from RO/distilled rather than adjusting unknown tap water — the math (RA, mash pH prediction, sulfate:chloride targeting) is only as good as the water composition numbers fed into it, and "measured zero plus known additions" is far more trustworthy input than "tap water, approximately."

That said, tap water is a perfectly workable starting point for many brewers, especially for styles that don't demand a specific profile, provided chlorine/chloramine is removed and the brewer has at least a rough idea of their water report (most utilities publish one; results across a given city can still vary by neighborhood and season).

## Style-appropriate water profiles: the classic historical city waters

Several historical European brewing cities are referenced by brewers as illustrative extremes, because their traditional beer styles evolved to suit (or, via water treatment, to sidestep) the local water rather than the water being adjusted to suit the beer:

- **Burton-on-Trent** (England) — famously very hard, high-sulfate water that shaped the crisp, dry bitterness of Burton pale ales and IPAs. Figures reported for a widely cited "Burton" profile: **calcium ~352 ppm, magnesium ~24 ppm, sodium ~24 ppm, sulfate ~607 ppm, chloride ~66 ppm, bicarbonate ~259 ppm.** *(Note: published Burton figures vary meaningfully by source — other citations put sulfate anywhere from roughly 600 to 800+ ppm and calcium around 300-350 ppm; this reflects genuine historical measurement variation and well-to-well differences, not a single agreed value. Treat "Burton water" as directionally very-high-sulfate/very-hard rather than a single precise target, and be aware that replicating the full historical figure produces sulfate levels some brewers find excessive or even mildly laxative.)*
- **Pilsen** (Czech Republic) — extremely soft, low-mineral water, which is why delicate, malt-and-noble-hop-balanced pilsners historically didn't rely on mineral character at all; any of that beer's character comes from malt and hop selection, not water. Cited figures: **calcium ~7 ppm, magnesium ~2 ppm, sodium ~2 ppm, sulfate ~6 ppm, chloride ~5 ppm, bicarbonate ~15 ppm** — about as close to distilled water as a natural municipal source gets.
- **Dublin** (Ireland) — moderately hard water with meaningful carbonate/bicarbonate alkalinity, historically well-suited to dark, roasty stouts because that alkalinity balances the natural acidity of the dark roasted malt (the Residual Alkalinity concept above, playing out at city scale). Cited figures: **calcium ~118 ppm, magnesium ~4 ppm, sodium ~12 ppm, sulfate ~55 ppm, chloride ~19 ppm, bicarbonate ~319 ppm.**
- **Munich** (Germany) — moderately hard with fairly high alkalinity and low sulfate, historically suited to malt-forward amber lagers (Munich dunkel, festbier) where a soft, round malt character is desired without hop crispness. Cited figures: **calcium ~75 ppm, magnesium ~18 ppm, sodium ~2 ppm, sulfate ~4 ppm, chloride ~1 ppm, bicarbonate ~262 ppm.**

These numbers are drawn from widely circulated historical water tables (the same tables underlie most brewing water calculators' built-in city profiles). Treat them as illustrative reference points for the *character* of water associated with a style, not as precision targets to hit exactly — actual historical well water varied by source and season, published tables disagree at the margins, and modern brewers rarely need to replicate a historical profile exactly to capture the character it implies.

## Sparge water chemistry

Sparge water needs separate consideration from mash (strike) water, because over-sparging can pull undesirable compounds out of the grain husks even when mash pH itself was fine:

- **The pH-6 upper bound.** As sparging continues and the sugar concentration in the runnings drops, the wort's own buffering capacity weakens and pH tends to drift upward. Widely cited guidance holds that if the pH in the lauter/sparge process climbs above roughly **6.0**, the risk of extracting tannins, silicates, and harsh husk compounds increases meaningfully — this is the practical ceiling brewers watch for, whether by direct pH measurement of the runnings or by treating sparge water itself to stay mildly acidic (commonly acidified to roughly pH 5.5-6.0 rather than left at tap-water-neutral or higher).
- **Gravity/volume cutoffs.** Because measuring runoff pH directly isn't always convenient, many brewers instead use a gravity cutoff as a practical proxy: stop sparging once the runnings drop to roughly **1.008-1.010 specific gravity**, since at that point there's little sugar left to extract and the risk/reward of continuing to sparge tips toward pulling astringent compounds rather than fermentable extract.
- **Temperature also matters alongside pH:** sparge water hotter than about 170°F (77°C) increases tannin extraction risk independent of pH, so both variables are usually controlled together.
- Practically, this means sparge water often benefits from the same light acidification (a small amount of acid, or simply using water with naturally low alkalinity) that mash water does, even though sparge water isn't buffered by grain acidity the way mash water is.

## Practical guidance: why / what / when / action / consequence

**Scenario: your tap water is chlorinated and you didn't treat it.**
- *Why it's a problem:* chlorine and especially chloramine react with phenolic compounds derived from malt during brewing to form chlorophenols.
- *What it produces:* a medicinal, "band-aid" or plastic-like off-flavor, detectable by most tasters at very low concentrations (some report thresholds under 10 parts per billion) and by some sources described as similar to a Chloraseptic-type medicinal taste.
- *When it shows up:* it can't be brewed around later — once chlorophenols form during mashing/boiling, they don't fade with conditioning or time; the flaw is permanent in that batch.
- *Action:* treat brewing water in advance with a Campden tablet (roughly 1 per 20 gallons / 75 L, or a quarter tablet per 5-gallon batch) or filter through activated carbon before use. Also apply the same treatment to any water used for cleaning/rinsing equipment that contacts wort or beer, not just brewing liquor.
- *Consequence if skipped:* an otherwise well-made batch can carry a persistent medicinal flavor that most drinkers immediately register as a flaw, with no fix after the fact.

**Scenario: you're brewing a hoppy IPA with naturally soft, low-mineral water (e.g., untreated RO, or a source resembling Pilsen-type water).**
- *Why it matters:* soft water has little calcium (which enzymes and yeast benefit from) and essentially no sulfate to accentuate hop bitterness, so the beer risks tasting flat, unstructured, and less "crisp" than the recipe intends, and the mash may also sit at a less-than-ideal pH without any hardness to help lower it.
- *What's likely without adjustment:* a muted, soft hop presentation — bitterness and hop character can taste rounded-off or muddled rather than the sharp, dry snap typically wanted in the style — plus a mash pH that may drift higher than ideal.
- *When to catch it:* before brew day, when building the water profile (or, for pH specifically, by measuring mash pH once dough-in is complete).
- *Action:* add gypsum (calcium sulfate) to raise both calcium and sulfate together, pushing the sulfate:chloride ratio toward the "hoppy/dry" end (comfortably above 1:1, commonly cited as high as several-to-one for aggressively hopped styles) while also helping mash pH settle into range.
- *Consequence if not adjusted:* the beer will very likely still ferment fine and taste "clean," but the hop character the recipe was designed around may come across as underwhelming or soft compared to intent — a real, if subtle, style-execution miss rather than a technical fault.

**Scenario: mash pH reads high — e.g., 5.8 or above (at room temperature, or the mash-temp equivalent).**
- *Why it happens:* usually a combination of high-alkalinity water and/or a grist that's too pale (not enough acidic crystal/roasted malt) to counteract that alkalinity — i.e., high Residual Alkalinity relative to what the grist can neutralize on its own.
- *What it does:* reduces enzyme efficiency somewhat (alpha- and beta-amylase both work best in the ~5.2-5.6 band, and activity drops off as pH rises further), can reduce extraction efficiency, increases the risk of tannin/husk-compound extraction (the same astringency risk discussed under sparge water, but now originating in the mash itself), and tends to push finished beer toward a duller, less crisp flavor with reduced clarity.
- *When to catch it:* ideally 10-15 minutes after dough-in, by pulling a sample and measuring with a calibrated pH meter or pH strips (cooled to near room temperature for an accurate reading, or accounting for the mash-temperature offset if measuring hot).
- *Action:* the standard corrective is a measured acid addition — food-grade **lactic acid** or **phosphoric acid** are the two most commonly used in homebrewing, added in small increments with re-measurement between additions (both are also sold as pre-formulated "pH stabilizer"/acid blend products). An alternative or complementary approach for dark beers specifically is leaning on the grist itself — adding a small amount of acidulated malt, or simply relying on the dark/roasted malts already in the recipe, since their natural acidity is exactly what offsets high-alkalinity water in traditional dark-beer brewing (the Dublin-water/stout pairing discussed above).
- *Consequence if not corrected:* the batch will very likely still ferment and finish as beer, but with measurably lower efficiency, a real risk of harsh/astringent tannin notes if sparging continues at that pH, and duller flavor and clarity than the same recipe would produce at a properly managed mash pH.
